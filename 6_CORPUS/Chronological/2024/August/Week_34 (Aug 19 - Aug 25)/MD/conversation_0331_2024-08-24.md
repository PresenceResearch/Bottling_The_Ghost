### Tools Needed:
1. **Mekugi-nuki** (a small brass or wooden tool used to remove the *mekugi* pins)
2. **Soft cloth or towel** (to protect the blade and your hands)

### Steps:

1. **Prepare a Safe Workspace**:

   - Ensure you have enough space to handle the sword safely.

2. **Remove the Mekugi (Pins)**:
   - Examine the handle (*tsuka*) and locate the small wooden or bamboo pins (*mekugi*) that secure the handle to the tang (*nakago*).
   - Using the *mekugi-nuki* tool, gently push the pins out of the handle. If the pins are tight, you can carefully tap the *mekugi-nuki* with a rubber mallet to loosen them. Be very gentle to avoid splitting the wood or damaging the pins.

3. **Slide off the Tsuba and Other Fittings**:
   - Once the *mekugi* pins are removed, the handle should slide off the tang. You may need to apply a bit of pressure, but do so gently.
   - After the handle is removed, the *tsuba* (guard) and other metal fittings (such as the fuchi, seppa, and kashira) can be slid off the tang.
   - If the fittings are stuck, avoid forcing them off. Sometimes gently tapping the handle with the palm of your hand or a soft mallet (rubber or wood) while holding the blade stationary can help loosen them.

4. **Handling the Blade**:
   - Be extremely cautious when handling the blade once the handle is removed, as it will be exposed and very sharp. Always hold the blade by the spine (non-sharp edge) or wrap it in a soft cloth to protect your hands and the blade itself.

### Important Tips:
- **Work Slowly and Carefully**: Disassembling a Japanese sword requires patience. Rushing the process can lead to damage.
- **Do Not Use Metal Tools on the Sword**: Avoid using metal tools directly on the sword fittings to prevent scratches or other damage.
- **Consult an Expert if Unsure**: If you’re unsure about any part of the process, it’s best to consult a professional or experienced sword handler to avoid any damage to the sword.

### Reassembly:

This process can vary slightly depending on the specific sword and its condition. If the sword is particularly old or valuable, or if the fittings are stuck, it may be wise to seek the assistance of a professional swordsmith or restorer.
