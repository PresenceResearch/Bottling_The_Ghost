Yes, **Thunderbolt** and **USB-C** share the **same connector type**, but they are not identical in functionality.

### Key Differences Between Thunderbolt and USB-C:
1. **Connector**:
   - Both use the same **USB-C physical connector**.

2. **Capabilities**:
   - **Thunderbolt (3 or 4)**:
     - Higher data transfer speeds (up to **40 Gbps**).
     - Supports **video output** for multiple monitors.
     - Allows **power delivery** (charging devices).
     - Can daisy-chain multiple devices.
   - **USB-C**:
     - Slower data transfer speeds (up to **10 Gbps** with USB 3.2 Gen 2).
     - Video output depends on whether the USB-C port supports **DisplayPort Alt Mode**.
     - May not support daisy-chaining.

3. **Markings**:
   - **Thunderbolt** ports are typically marked with a **lightning bolt icon**.
   - USB-C ports may or may not have markings, but they won't include the Thunderbolt icon unless they support Thunderbolt.

---

### Thunderbolt on Your Mac Mini (2023 M2)
Your **Mac Mini with M2** has **two Thunderbolt/USB 4 ports**, which:
- Use the USB-C connector.
- Support Thunderbolt functionality (e.g., high-speed data and external displays).

So, you can use **Thunderbolt devices** or **USB-C accessories** interchangeably with these ports, but you'll get better performance with Thunderbolt-compatible hardware.

A **DisplayPort** is a digital display interface primarily used to connect a computer to monitors or other display devices. It is a standard designed to handle high-resolution video and audio. Here's what you need to know:

---

### **Key Features of DisplayPort**
1. **High Resolution and Refresh Rates**:
   - Supports resolutions up to **8K at 60Hz** or **4K at 120Hz** (depending on the version).
   - Ideal for high-performance monitors, gaming, and professional editing.

2. **Audio and Video**:
   - Transmits both video and audio over a single cable, similar to HDMI.

3. **Shape and Connector**:
   - The standard **DisplayPort connector** is a rectangular shape with one corner slightly beveled.
   - **Mini DisplayPort** is a smaller version often found on older Macs and laptops.

4. **Daisy-Chaining**:
   - Many DisplayPort monitors support **daisy-chaining**, meaning you can connect multiple monitors to a single DisplayPort output (if both the monitors and the computer support this).

5. **Adaptive Sync**:
   - DisplayPort supports **Adaptive Sync technologies**, such as AMD FreeSync and NVIDIA G-Sync, for smoother gaming performance.

---

### **DisplayPort vs. HDMI**
| **Feature**            | **DisplayPort**                  | **HDMI**                     |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Primary Use**         | Computers and professional displays | Home entertainment (TVs, consoles) |
| **Resolution/Refresh**  | Up to 8K/60Hz (or higher)        | Up to 4K/120Hz, 8K with HDMI 2.1 |
| **Daisy-Chaining**      | Supported                       | Not supported                |
| **Connector**           | Rectangular with a beveled edge | Flat with angled corners     |

---

### **How It’s Used**
- **Direct Connection**:
   - Computers with a DisplayPort output connect to monitors with DisplayPort inputs using a **DisplayPort cable**.
- **Adapters**:
   - DisplayPort to HDMI: If your monitor only has HDMI.
   - DisplayPort to USB-C: To connect to modern laptops or monitors.
   - DisplayPort to VGA/DVI: For older monitors (though not recommended).

---

### **Does Your Mac Mini Use DisplayPort?**
Your Mac Mini does not have a physical DisplayPort. However:
- The **Thunderbolt/USB-C ports** on your Mac Mini support **DisplayPort output** using a **USB-C to DisplayPort cable or adapter**.

This makes DisplayPort an excellent choice for connecting high-resolution monitors or those requiring high refresh rates.
