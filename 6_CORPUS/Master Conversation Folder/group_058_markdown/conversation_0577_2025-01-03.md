1. **Cargo-Handling Equipment**:

   - Forklifts
   - Top handlers
   - Side picks

   - Heavy-duty trucks used for short-distance cargo transport to and from ports.

3. **Locomotives and Railcar Movers**:
   - Battery-electric or hydrogen-powered rail equipment used for moving railcars at ports.

4. **Harbor Craft and Other Vessels**:
   - Examples include:
     - Tugboats
     - Ferries
     - Tribal and commercial fishing vessels
     - Workboats
     - Dredges
     - Pilot boats
     - Barges

### Features of ZE Equipment:
- **Battery-Electric Vehicles (BEVs)**: Use rechargeable batteries to store electricity.
- **Hydrogen Fuel Cell Vehicles (HFCVs)**: Use hydrogen gas to generate electricity through fuel cells.
- **Zero Tailpipe Emissions**: No release of pollutants like particulate matter (PM), nitrogen oxides (NOx), or carbon dioxide (CO2).

### Key Requirements:
- ZE equipment under this program must directly serve ports and operate primarily within port facilities or nearby areas.
- Fully automated or hybrid vehicles (partially powered by internal combustion engines) are not considered ZE unless the non-ZE component is for emergency use only.

The key difference between **direct connection** and **net metered** solar systems lies in how the energy generated by the solar panels is used and integrated with the electrical grid. Here's a breakdown:

---

### **Direct Connection**
A **direct connection** means the solar system is physically and electrically connected to the equipment or facility it powers, without relying on the utility grid for energy distribution.

#### **How it Works**:
- **Solar energy flows directly** to the equipment (e.g., EV chargers) being powered.
- Excess solar energy that is not used is often stored in **on-site batteries** or is lost if no storage is available.
- The equipment draws electricity directly from the solar panels when sunlight is available, and from the grid or batteries when solar isn’t producing enough power.

#### **Advantages**:
1. **Full utilization of solar energy** on-site.
2. **Immediate emissions reductions**, as the equipment uses renewable energy directly.
3. Does not rely on net metering policies or grid interactions.

#### **Challenges**:
- Requires careful sizing to match the equipment's energy needs.
- May require battery storage to provide power when solar production is low (e.g., at night).

---

### **Net Metered Solar**
A **net-metered** solar system is connected to the **utility grid**. Energy produced by the solar system is either consumed on-site or exported to the grid, depending on the energy demand and solar production at any given time.

#### **How it Works**:
- Solar energy powers on-site equipment (e.g., EV chargers) **first**. Any surplus is sent back to the grid.
- When on-site demand exceeds solar production (e.g., at night), the equipment draws power from the grid.
- Net metering allows the user to "bank" energy credits for the excess electricity sent to the grid. These credits offset the cost of energy drawn from the grid later.

#### **Advantages**:
1. **Energy credits** help offset costs, making the system financially viable even when solar production doesn’t match demand.
2. Simplifies installation by leveraging the grid as a backup.
3. No need for large on-site battery storage (though batteries can still enhance the system).

#### **Challenges**:
1. Emissions reductions are **indirect**, as the grid acts as an intermediary. The system doesn’t guarantee that the grid is using clean energy when you draw from it.
2. Reliant on local/state **net metering policies**, which can vary widely. For example:
   - Some utilities provide 1:1 credit for energy sent back to the grid.
   - Others may offer lower rates or cap the amount of energy eligible for net metering.

---

### **Comparison of Key Differences**

| Feature                  | **Direct Connection**                                  | **Net Metered**                                       |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| **Energy Use**           | Energy is used directly by the equipment or stored.   | Energy is used on-site first; excess goes to the grid. |
| **Reliance on Grid**     | Minimal (only for backup, if connected at all).       | Relies on the grid for exporting excess and importing energy. |
| **Emissions Reductions** | Immediate and direct.                                 | Indirect; depends on grid policies and energy mix.    |
| **Excess Energy**        | Requires batteries to store or may be wasted.         | Exported to the grid for credits (if allowed).        |
| **Policy Dependency**    | No reliance on net metering policies.                 | Dependent on net metering rules and rates.            |
| **System Complexity**    | May require additional storage solutions.             | Simpler setup without battery storage.                |

---

### **Which Is Better for the Clean Ports Program?**
- **Direct Connection** is more closely aligned with the program’s goal of directly reducing emissions, as it ensures the solar energy is used exclusively for port-related ZE infrastructure.
- **Net Metered** systems are also eligible but require documentation showing that the energy offsets the increased electricity demand (e.g., for EV chargers). Emissions reductions are less direct and more dependent on the grid's energy mix.

---

### **Summary**:
- **Direct Connection**: Solar energy goes straight to the equipment, ensuring immediate and direct emissions reductions.
- **Net Metered**: Solar energy offsets grid electricity use, with emissions reductions depending on the local grid mix and policies.

If a solar project is funded using **federal grant money**, it may still be eligible for the **Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC)**, but **only for the portion of the project not funded by the grant**. Federal tax law prohibits "double-dipping," meaning you can't claim a tax credit on expenses already covered by federal funds.

---

### **How It Works:**
1. **Grant-Funded Portion Is Excluded**:
   - The ITC applies only to the portion of the project paid for with **non-federal funds** (e.g., private capital, loans, state or local grants, or personal/business funds).
   - For example, if 50% of the project is funded by a federal grant, only the remaining 50% of the costs can qualify for the ITC.

2. **Non-Federal Match Counts**:
   - If the project includes matching funds (required or voluntary), these non-federal funds are eligible for the tax credit.

3. **Documentation**:
   - You must clearly document the funding sources for the project to ensure compliance with tax rules.

---

### **Example**:
Let’s say a solar system costs $1 million, and:
- A federal grant covers **60%** ($600,000).
- Non-federal sources cover **40%** ($400,000).

Only the $400,000 funded with non-federal dollars is eligible for the ITC. At a 30% credit rate:
- Tax credit = $400,000 × 30% = **$120,000**.

---

### **What About State/Local Grants?**
State or local grants **do not disqualify** the project from claiming the ITC. However:
- The IRS may treat some state grants as income, which could have tax implications for the recipient.

1. **Direct Pay Option** (for tax-exempt entities like nonprofits, tribes, or municipalities):

2. **Bonus Credits**:
   - Projects in **disadvantaged communities**, using **domestically produced components**, or meeting **prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements** may qualify for additional credits beyond the base 30%.

If your project uses federal grant money, you can still claim the ITC for the **non-grant-funded portion** of the project costs.
